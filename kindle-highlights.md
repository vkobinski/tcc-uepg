## 01 — Automation Bias Cockpits (Mosier, 1998)

- "The main goal is to improve the reliability and quality of human decision-making at work or in everyday life."
- "the presence of DSSs may foster automation bias"
- "The devices themselves had to be operated and monitored by the very humans whose caprice they were designed to avoid."
- "The devices themselves had the potential for generating errors that could result in accidents."
- "commission errors occur when decision makers take inappropriate action because they over-attend to information"
- "Given this, automation bias in terms of omission errors can be understood as a consequence of overreliance on a DSS, whereas commission errors reflect a sort of overcompliance with a DSS."
- "The 'cognitive miser' perspective suggests that people tend to take the road of least cognitive effort and may diffuse responsibility for decision tasks to automated aids, resulting in incomplete cross-checking of available information."
- "Second, operators may inattentively process information that contradicts automated directives, analogous to a 'looking but not seeing' or inattentional blindness effect"
- "a phenomenon labelled 'phantom memory'"
- "information from less automated sources because they believe the automated decision support system is more likely correct"
- "ENGINE FIRE prompted the pilots to shut down the wrong engine"
- "a total of 75% of pilots followed the wrong recommendation of the decision-aid."
- "the frequency of omission errors was 55% - and 100% of the pilots decided to shut down the engine in response to the erroneous EICAS alert."
- "67% of the pilots reported that they had seen at least one other indication supporting the EICAS fire alert which in fact was not there."
- "In contrast only 3% of the critical events were missed by a control group performing the task without automation support."
- "presence of an automated support for decisions made pilots less likely to seek and assess information from other [sources]"
- "but rather a delayed detection and response to contradictory information leaving insufficient time to respond to a critical situation"
- "Correct CDSS advices increased the number of correct diagnoses after consultation of the aid by 2-21%. However, at the same time, incorrect advice led clinicians in 6%-11% of cases to commit commission errors, that is, to change their initially correct diagnoses into wrong ones after consulting the false advice of the aid, which reduced the net gain effects of the CDSS significantly."
- "Participants who made omission errors in this study reported lower cognitive load compared with those who did not."
- "the film readers in this study tended to take the absence of a computer prompt as evidence for the absence of cancer. The authors interpreted this finding as evidence for automation bias, possibly due to a reduced vigilance when supported by a CAD."
- "In case of correct diagnoses and advice provided by AFIRA, operators' performance improved and became close to perfect. However, in case of an incorrect diagnosis provided by the aid, converging evidence for automation bias was found in all studies."
- "80% of operators committing a typical commission error"
- "erroneously following such automated aids has already contributed to fatal military decisions, including friendly fires during the Iraq War."
- "found an inverse relationship between trust and monitoring performance."
- "operators adjust their trust in automation in line with its performance"
- "This might also explain in part why professionals working with highly reliable systems are equally as susceptible to automation bias as novices"
- "there is evidence that individuals differ in their proneness to rely too much on automated systems. This individual tendency has been referred to as complacency potential"
- "demonstrated that the imposition of pre-decisional accountability... resulted in lower rates of omission and commission errors compared non-accountable student participants"
- "pilots who reported an internalized perception of 'accountability' for their performance and strategies of interaction with automation were significantly more likely to double-check automated cues"

## 03 — AI Tools Cognitive Offloading (Gerlich, 2025)

- "The findings revealed a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by increased cognitive offloading. Younger participants exhibited higher dependence on AI tools and lower critical thinking scores compared to older participants."
- "emphasising the need for educational strategies that promote critical engagement with AI technologies."
- "growing evidence shows that over-reliance on these tools can lead to cognitive offloading."
- "involves using external tools to reduce the cognitive load on an individual's working memory."
- "Sparrow et al. demonstrated that the availability of information through search engines can affect memory retention and the inclination to process information deeply."
- "This could result in a workforce that is highly efficient, yet potentially less capable of independent problem-solving and critical evaluation."
- "Critical thinking is integral to cognitive development, enabling individuals to process information more effectively and engage in reflective thought."
- "Cognitive offloading refers to the externalisation of cognitive processes, often involving tools or external agents, such as notes, calculators, or digital tools like AI, to reduce cognitive load"
- "In some cases, however, extensive reliance on external tools—particularly AI—may reduce the need for deep cognitive involvement, potentially affecting critical thinking."
- "cognitive offloading can be seen as a beneficial strategy for managing cognitive load and enhancing productivity."
- "'Google effect', suggesting that the availability of information at our fingertips reduces the need for internal memory retention."
- "people are more likely to remember where to find information rather than the information itself."
- "AI-driven devices and applications can also fragment attention and reduce the ability to focus on a single task for extended periods."
- "AI tools take over these tasks, individuals may become less proficient in developing and applying their own problem-solving strategies, leading to a decline in cognitive flexibility and creativity."
- "AI tools can automate routine and complex tasks, thereby reducing cognitive load and freeing up cognitive resources for higher-order thinking."
- "One concern is the risk of cognitive dependence, where individuals become overly reliant on AI tools for routine and complex tasks."
- "This 'black box' problem can reduce critical engagement and accountability, as individuals may blindly trust AI recommendations without questioning or evaluating them"
- "AI tools might inadvertently reinforce biases and limit exposure to diverse perspectives. Algorithms used by AI systems can create echo chambers by recommending content that aligns with users' existing beliefs, thereby reducing exposure to contrasting viewpoints."
- "algorithmic bias can hinder critical evaluation by encouraging confirmation bias and reducing critical scrutiny of information"
- "The opaqueness of AI processes often leads users to accept AI-generated conclusions without scrutiny, risking diminished engagement and reliance on automated inferences."
- "While cognitive offloading can free up cognitive resources, there is concern that it may lead to a reduction in cognitive effort, fostering what some researchers refer to as 'cognitive laziness'"
- "workers who rely heavily on AI-driven decision-support systems may struggle to make decisions independently when these systems are unavailable"
- "While AI tools can improve basic skill acquisition, they may not foster the deep analytical thinking required for applying these skills in novel or complex situations"
- "While these tools can help students refine their technical skills, they may not foster the deep analytical thinking required for developing arguments and critical evaluation."
